Core Insight

Bernstein's concepts of vertical and horizontal knowledge structures offer a powerful lens for understanding how academic disciplines organise knowledge, which directly informs the academic literacies model's focus on the social practices of reading and writing.

Bernstein's Core Concepts

Vertical Knowledge Structures

Ladder representing cumulative, hierarchical knowledge structure.

Bernstein's concept of vertical knowledge structures describes knowledge organised hierarchically, where concepts build systematically upon previous ones. This creates a cumulative, integrated framework typical in disciplines like physics or mathematics, where foundational principles underpin more complex theories.

Applying this to academic literacies, vertical structures correspond to disciplines where writing practices are highly codified and cumulative. Students must master specific genres and conventions that build upon each other. The academic literacies model helps reveal how explicit rules shape the literacy practices students must learn.

In vertical structures, knowledge is cumulative, building upon itself in a coherent, integrated way.
Hierarchy Cumulative Integration Sciences
Characterised by systematic progression and integration of concepts.

Horizontal Knowledge Structures

Sunrise representing diverse, segmented knowledge structure.

Bernstein's horizontal knowledge structures are segmented, consisting of distinct, often unrelated approaches or languages within a field. These lack a single integrating framework, leading to weaker classification and framing. This is typical in fields like the humanities.

The academic literacies model is particularly valuable for understanding horizontal structures. It highlights how students must navigate diverse, often competing genres and discourses within these fields. The model emphasises the situated nature of literacy practices, where meaning, identity, and power are negotiated differently in each segment.

In horizontal structures, knowledge areas exist side-by-side, often incommensurable with each other.
Segmentation Parallel Incommensurable Humanities
Characterised by distinct, parallel, and often unrelated knowledge areas.

Application & Practice

Navigating Genres and Modes

Bernstein's framework helps us understand the complexity of student experience in navigating academic literacies. The requirement to switch between genres and modes reflects the underlying structure of the knowledge domain.

The academic literacies model emphasises that these shifts are not merely technical. Each genre and mode embodies different social meanings, identities, and power relations. Students must learn not just the forms, but the social practices embedded within them.

Students must develop awareness of the different qualities associated with various genres and modes as they move between communication contexts in academic work.
Genre Mode Transformation Multimodality
Recognising the complex shifts students make between academic communication forms.

Pedagogy & Design Frame

Bernstein's concepts are crucial for curriculum design and pedagogy. Recognising whether a subject area is predominantly vertical or horizontal helps educators anticipate the literacy challenges students will face.

Lea and Street's academic literacies model operationalises this by advocating for a 'design frame' that makes the social and epistemological nature of academic tasks explicit. This moves beyond deficit models to a more nuanced understanding of student learning.

Treating such students as collaborators in the development of the academic literacies necessary... can perhaps offer a different and more supportive route.
Pedagogy Design Frame Curriculum Recontextualisation
Applying the model's principles to create more supportive learning environments.

Multimodal Meaning-Making

In academic settings, meaning is rarely constructed through language alone. The academic literacies model recognises that students engage with and produce texts that combine linguistic, visual, gestural, and spatial elements.

For instance, a student might use a combination of words, graphs, charts, images, and diagrams in a research report. Understanding the interplay between these different modes is essential for effective academic communication.

Academic literacy is enriched when we recognise and value diverse knowledge traditions and communication practices across cultural contexts.

Understanding the role of different modes in constructing academic meaning.
