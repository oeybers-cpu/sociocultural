Social Concept

Academic literacy is not a set of isolated skills, but a social practice deeply embedded in institutional power structures. In South Africa, English dominance in higher education creates social hierarchies that can marginalise students whose home languages are African.

Leveraging the social concept means recognising that literacy develops through collaboration and participation. It challenges autonomous models by foregrounding how power and authority shape what "counts" as legitimate academic knowledge.

Cultural Concept

Academic literacy is profoundly shaped by cultural context. In post-apartheid South Africa, this involves valuing the rich cultural capital, including Indigenous Knowledge Systems like Ma'at traditions, found in students' home communities and languages.

The cultural concept challenges Western-centric epistemologies. Integrating these cultural resources into pedagogy is essential for epistemic decolonisation, enabling students to demonstrate mastery through their full linguistic and cultural repertoires.

Theoretical Framework

Sociocultural Linguistics

This field studies language in its sociocultural context, moving beyond quantitative methods to embrace perspectives from anthropology and discourse analysis. It views language as a tool mediating between individual thought and societal structures.

It provides the theoretical bedrock for understanding how social and cultural factors fundamentally shape communication and meaning-making in academic settings.

Academic Literacies Model

Lea and Street's model evolved from skills-based approaches to socially situated understandings. It addresses meaning-making, identity, and power, foregrounding the institutional nature of what "counts" as legitimate knowledge.

This model is crucial for moving beyond viewing literacy as a neutral skill, instead recognising it as a practice embedded in specific academic cultures and power dynamics.

Language Policy

South Africa's policy promotes multilingualism, yet English dominates higher education. This creates a barrier for students whose home languages are African, forcing them to navigate a system that devalues their first language.

The gap between policy and practice perpetuates inequalities, undermining the goal of truly inclusive education and limiting students' full potential in their disciplines.

Social Dynamics

English is associated with prestige, professional success, and global access, creating pressure for students to adopt it. This dynamic reinforces the idea that academic success requires linguistic assimilation.

Students must constantly negotiate their identities, balancing their cultural heritage with the perceived advantages of English, which can be emotionally and academically taxing.

Models of Literacy

Critique of Autonomy

The autonomous model views literacy as a neutral, technical skill, ignoring its social and cultural context. This is theoretically flawed and pedagogically harmful, as it fails to account for the diverse ways students make meaning.

Practices like monolingual tests prevent students from demonstrating true disciplinary mastery, instead measuring their ability to conform to a single, culturally specific standard.

Ideological Empowerment

Street's ideological model recognises literacy as embedded in power relations. This empowers students by validating their home languages and cultural knowledge as legitimate tools for academic inquiry and critical thought.

By honouring students' diverse identities, this approach fosters an environment where they can leverage their full repertoire of skills to succeed meaningfully in their chosen disciplines.

Valuing Cultural Capital

Students arrive at university with rich cultural capital and literacy practices from their communities. A sociocultural framework recognises this diversity as an asset to be valued and built upon, not replaced or remediated.

This approach respects students' existing knowledge and provides a foundation for integrating new academic literacies in a way that is personally and culturally relevant.

Literacy as Social Practice

The Academic Literacies as Social Practices framework emphasises that learning occurs through interaction and cultural mediation. Students' multiple identities require pedagogies that acknowledge their complex linguistic repertoires.

By framing literacy development as a social process, this model creates inclusive classrooms where collaborative meaning-making is central to achieving disciplinary understanding.

Indigenous Pedagogies

Ma'at and Tewahedo

Indigenous pedagogies like Ma'at (emphasising truth and balance) and Tewahedo (signifying unity) provide collaborative learning frameworks. These align perfectly with social practice theories of literacy and holistic education.

Integrating these traditions offers structured, culturally resonant methods for fostering critical thinking, ethical reasoning, and community-based knowledge construction in the classroom.

Oral Traditions as Literacy

isiXhosa and isiZulu folktales and songs reveal sophisticated literacy instruction embedded in indigenous practices. They feature dialogical interaction, integrated knowledge forms, and multiliteracy development.

These traditions challenge Western assumptions, demonstrating that African epistemologies offer rich, valid, and effective pathways for developing the complex communication skills needed for academic success.

Translanguaging

Translanguaging leverages students' full linguistic repertoires, allowing them to use all their languages as integrated resources for learning. This approach shows promise for simultaneous African language development and academic success.

It validates students' identities and provides flexible, powerful tools for comprehension, analysis, and expression, leading to deeper engagement with disciplinary content.

Inclusive African Theory (ATAL)

The ATAL framework moves beyond autonomous constructs to value students' evolving identities and cultural intelligences. It integrates African epistemologies to inspire new, more equitable assessment methods.

This theory enables students to demonstrate disciplinary mastery across multiple vocabularies and modes of expression, ensuring their success is measured by their knowledge, not their linguistic conformity.

Current Challenges

Key challenges include institutional resistance to change, limited resources for African language development, and persistent ideological preferences for English-medium instruction. These factors create significant barriers to transformation.

Overcoming these requires sustained advocacy, resource allocation, and a fundamental shift in institutional culture to value linguistic diversity as an academic asset.

Emerging Opportunities

Policies like the BELA Act signal a shift towards mother-tongue-based bilingual education. The establishment of African language departments in 92% of universities provides crucial infrastructure for this transformation.

Research into responsive curricula and innovative pedagogies offers practical pathways for leveraging these opportunities to create truly inclusive and effective learning environments.
