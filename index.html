The relationship between sociocultural linguistics and academic literacy development in post-apartheid South Africa is fundamentally transformative, challenging traditional autonomous models of literacy whilst foregrounding the cultural, social, and political dimensions of language learning in higher education contexts.

This relationship represents a paradigm shift from viewing literacy as a neutral, technical skill towards understanding it as a socially embedded practice that reflects and shapes power relations, cultural identities, and knowledge systems within academic institutions.

Social Concept

Academic literacy is not a set of isolated skills, but a social practice deeply embedded in institutional power structures and community interactions. In the South African context, this means understanding how language ideologies and the dominance of English in higher education create social hierarchies that can marginalise students whose home languages are African.

Leveraging the social concept means recognising that literacy development happens through collaboration, dialogue, and participation in academic communities. It challenges autonomous models by foregrounding how power, identity, and authority shape what "counts" as legitimate academic knowledge and expression.

Cultural Concept

Academic literacy is profoundly shaped by cultural context and the diverse knowledge systems students bring with them. In post-apartheid South Africa, this involves valuing the rich cultural capital, including Indigenous Knowledge Systems (IKS) like Ma'at and Tewahedo traditions, found in students' home communities and languages such as isiXhosa and isiZulu.

The cultural concept challenges Western-centric epistemologies by asserting that African ways of knowing, often transmitted through oral traditions and multiliteracies, are valid and sophisticated. Integrating these cultural resources into pedagogy is essential for epistemic decolonisation and for enabling students to demonstrate mastery through their full linguistic and cultural repertoires.

Sociocultural Linguistics

Sociocultural linguistics encompasses a broad range of theories and methods for studying language in its sociocultural context, moving beyond narrow quantitative sociolinguistic approaches to embrace transdisciplinary perspectives from linguistic anthropology, discourse analysis, and sociology of language. This field emphasises how language functions as a mediating tool that bridges individual cognitive processes with wider cultural and societal structures.

Academic Literacy Development

Academic literacy development, particularly through Lea and Street's influential three-model framework, has evolved from technical skills-based approaches towards more socially situated understandings. The academic literacies model specifically addresses meaning-making, identity, power and authority, foregrounding the institutional nature of what "counts" as knowledge in particular contexts.

The South African Context: Language and Power

Multilingual
Twelve Official Languages
English Hegemony
Linguistic diversity in South Africa

In post-apartheid South Africa's multilingual society with twelve official languages, the relationship between sociocultural linguistics and academic literacy development is particularly complex. Despite constitutional provisions promoting multilingualism, English and Afrikaans remain dominant in higher education, whilst more than 76% of the population speaks African languages as their home languages.

This linguistic landscape reflects what scholars term a "gridlock of collusion" between political elites and African-language speakers who seek the perceived benefits of English-medium education. The persistence of English hegemony demonstrates how language ideologies—socially constructed beliefs about language value and legitimacy—continue to shape educational practices.

Language Policy

South Africa's language policy aims to promote multilingualism, yet in practice, English dominates higher education. This creates a significant barrier for students whose home languages are African, forcing them to navigate a system where their first language is not valued or utilised.

This discrepancy between policy and practice perpetuates inequalities and undermines the goal of inclusive education.

Social Dynamics

The social dynamics surrounding language use in academia are deeply intertwined with perceptions of prestige and power. English is often associated with upward mobility, professional success, and access to global knowledge, creating a powerful incentive for students to adopt it, even at the expense of their native tongues.

The image depicts the tension between home languages and academic English. Students must constantly negotiate their identities, balancing the cultural richness of their home languages with the perceived advantages of English. This dynamic reinforces the idea that academic success is tied to linguistic assimilation rather than equitable access to knowledge.

Rethinking Autonomous Models

Ideological Model
Power Relations
Epistemic Injustice
Contrast between autonomous and ideological models of literacy

The sociocultural approach fundamentally opposes autonomous models of literacy that view reading and writing as neutral, decontextualised technical skills. Street's ideological model of literacy, central to New Literacy Studies, recognises that literacy practices are always embedded in power relations and cultural contexts.

In South Africa, autonomous approaches manifest in discriminatory practices such as monolingual multiple-choice tests that prevent students from demonstrating disciplinary mastery whilst marginalising diverse knowledge production tools. These approaches perpetuate what researchers call "epistemic injustice" by privileging Global North epistemologies over African knowledge systems.

Students struggling with decontextualised academic tasks

A Critical Reassessment for Student Success

A detailed critique of the autonomous model reveals its fundamental limitations: it ignores the rich cultural and linguistic resources students bring to the classroom, treating literacy as a context-independent skill that can be uniformly measured. This is not only theoretically flawed but pedagogically harmful. A context-independent view of literacy is problematic because it fails to account for the diverse ways students make meaning and demonstrate understanding.

The ideological model, in contrast, has a profound and positive real-world impact. By acknowledging that all literacy practices are situated within specific social and cultural contexts, it empowers students. It validates their home languages and cultural knowledge as legitimate tools for academic inquiry. This approach disempowers exclusionary practices and instead fosters an environment where students can leverage their full repertoire of skills to succeed in their chosen disciplines. True disciplinary mastery is not about conforming to a single, monolingual standard; it is about developing the ability to think critically, communicate effectively, and contribute meaningfully within a field—goals that are best achieved by honouring and integrating students' diverse identities and ways of knowing.

Traditional African storytelling and knowledge transmission

A critical dimension of this relationship involves integrating Indigenous Knowledge Systems (IKS) into academic literacy development. Research demonstrates how indigenous pedagogies, such as Ma'at and Tewahedo traditions, provide collaborative learning frameworks that align with contemporary social practice theories of literacy.

Studies of isiXhosa and isiZulu folktales and traditional songs reveal sophisticated literacy instruction embedded in indigenous practices, including dialogical interaction, integrated knowledge forms, and multiliteracy development. These findings challenge Western-centric assumptions about literacy development and demonstrate the wealth of African epistemological resources.

The integration of sociocultural linguistics with academic literacy development offers transformative possibilities for South African higher education. Translanguaging approaches that leverage students' full linguistic repertoires show promise for simultaneous African language development and academic success.

The proposed Inclusive African Theory of Academic Literacy (ATAL) argues for moving beyond autonomous constructs towards approaches that value students' evolving identities and cultural intelligences. This framework integrates students' cultures and African epistemologies to inspire new assessment methods and enable demonstration of disciplinary mastery across multiple vocabularies.

Challenges and Opportunities

BELA Act
Institutional Resistance
Mother-tongue Education
University infrastructure supporting multilingual education

Current challenges include institutional resistance to linguistic transformation, limited resources for African language development, and persistent ideological preferences for English-medium instruction. However, recent policy developments such as the BELA Act signal potential shifts towards mother-tongue-based bilingual education.

The establishment of African language departments in 92% of South African universities, alongside language units in 58% of institutions, provides infrastructure for transformation. Critical research explores alternatives to "one-size-fits-all" solutions, emphasising curriculum responsiveness to diverse student needs.
